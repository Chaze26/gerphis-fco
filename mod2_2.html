Kalinga Tattooing


Meet Apo Whang-od, the living legend and last mambabatok of the Philippines. With her steady hands and ancestral wisdom, she brings to life the ancient art of hand-tapped tattoos, preserving her cultural heritage and inspiring generations with her craft.

A beacon of tradition and resilience, her legacy transcends time, leaving an indelible mark on the hearts of those who encounter her.

Description

Tattooing in Kalinga is traditionally called “batek” or “batuk”. It has an Austronesian origin from the word “betik”. The term was derived from the sound of the tapping of the stick on the tattoo instrument to pierce the skin. The word tik means “to hit slowly”.

Tattoo work in Kalinga is done by a tattoo practitioner called “manbatok”.

There are two kinds of tattoo practitioners: the one who travels to other villages and the other who remains in their own village.

Ink is made from the charcoal powder or soot scraped from underneath a clay pot.

A known alternative tattoo ink is scraped from the bottom of an aluminum pan used for daily cooking.

Sugarcane soot and water are added to the fine charcoal powder and stirred to a desired thickness.

The main tattoo instrument is called the gisi and is hand-tapped using a pat-ik, a wooden stick. The gisi is a stick with a lemon thorn that pierces the skin, creating the tattoo.

Design and
Symbolism

The different kinds of tattoo designs can be distinguished by grades or “badges of honor”, similar to the badges worn by present-day military soldiers.  

One common design is the whiing or chest tattoo. Aiyung tattoo can be found on both cheeks of men but are considered very rare.

IMPORTANCE

Tattooing among the Kalinga people is integral to their cultural identity and social status. Designs of tattoos carry specific meanings known only to the community members. Heavily tattooed individuals, such as pangat (men) and daughters of kadangyan (women), are highly respected in society. These tattoos also play a crucial role in rites of passage marking transitions in life stages: rites of separation, transition, and incorporation.  

Rites of Separation

In rites of separation, women receive Lin-lingao tattoos for protection, especially when married or pregnant.



Rites of Transition

Rites of transition include binulubid and bikking tattoos for men, symbolizing bravery and adulthood, and Nirafarafat and Inufu-ufug for women, indicating readiness for marriage and motherhood.

Rites of Incorporation

In rites of incorporation, warriors receive badges of honor tattoos, while women receive tattoos signifying acceptance into adulthood and eligibility for marriage and childbirth.
