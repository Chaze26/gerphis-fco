Filipino livelihood during the American Colonization 

During the American colonization of the Philippines, Filipino livelihoods saw a shift towards cash crop cultivation and urban employment alongside persistent challenges of land dispossession and labor exploitation.

1900 - 1909

Free Trade Relations

The American-imposed free trade relations initially favored the Philippines with a 25% reduction on goods coming from the country. Limited access to American markets led to moderate growth in Philippine foreign trade.

1910 - 1914

Payne-Aldrich Tariff Act

The Payne-Aldrich Tariff Act established partial free trade relations between the Philippines and the United States, allowing certain exports duty-free entry into American markets.

1914 - 1918

Balance of Trade

Despite disruptions caused by World War I, the Philippines maintained a favorable balance of trade.

1930 - 1935

Tydings-McDuffie Independence Act of 1934

Restrictions on Philippine exports were imposed by the Tydings-McDuffie Independence Act of 1934, disrupting the economy and highlighting the risks of dependence on American markets.

Transportation

At the end of the Spanish period, the Philippines had less than 1,000 miles of road. By 1935, the mileage increased significantly to 12,912 miles. The government's acquisition of the Manila-Dagupan Railway Company facilitated the expansion, connecting far-flung provinces to Manila. Interisland shipping was encouraged under American rule. Over a hundred ports were opened to domestic shipping in 1902, and a law passed in 1923 mandated that only locally-owned ships could engage in inter island shipping.

Communication

Telephone lines were introduced in Manila in 1905, and radio-telephone service began in 1933. Towns and provincial capitals were linked via telephones, telegraph lines, and radio. Mail offices throughout the country handled ordinary mail, telegrams, money orders, and air mail letters and packages by 1935.
