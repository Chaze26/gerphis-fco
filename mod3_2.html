Filipino livelihood during the Japanese Colonization 

During the Japanese colonization of the Philippines, Filipino livelihoods underwent significant changes as they adapted to new economic and social conditions imposed by the occupying forces.

The Economy During the Japanese Occupation

The Economy of the Philippines during the Japanese occupation suffered. Economic activities were toned down due to other limitations set by the Japanese and also due to the confiscation of transportation means such as cars, trucks and even horses.  

Exploitation of Medicine and Food

During the Japanese occupation, captured Filipino soldiers were amassed in Capas. Food and medicine were scarce leading to many of the Filipino soldiers to death brought upon by hunger and diseases. As a result of this, the Japanese allowed the family,friends and relatives of the soldiers to be able to send food and medicine.

However, due to immoral fellow Filipino army physicians, who were supposed to give the medicine and food, sold the medicines at a high price in Philippine pesos such as sulfathiazole,sulfanilamide, and quinine instead of giving them to the injured soldiers. This grim event was named the “Tragedy of Capas”.

Economic Disruption of Japanese Cotton Planters

The Japanese during the war favored the planting of cotton over rice. This decreased the production of rice as haciendas were filled with cotton. This decrease in supply of rice increased the demand of rice resulting in the increasing price of rice. The urban population of Manila did not have land to plant rice and had to rely on buying rice from the countryside.

However, due to the increased price of rice, many of the population in Manila were unable to afford buying rice. This resulted in the urban population selling their expensive furniture in exchange for rice. This led to the temporary rise in living standard of the farmers.

Economy of Substitutes

As the Japanese occupied the Philippines, the economy was hampered. Some of the basic commodities that were previously available were now scarce and expensive. This led Filipinos to innovate and find substitutes for those basic commodities.  

An example of this is coffee and tea where instead of coffee beans toasted rice and corn were substituted. For tea, boiled mango leaves were also used as a substitute. American Cigarettes were also scarce, this made its price increase leading many Filipinos to manufacture their own cigarettes using dried papaya leaves,newspaper, and books.
