Tattooing and Body Piercing

Ink and Steel: Exploring Tattooing and Body Piercing in Pre-Colonial Filipino Culture

Penis Pins

A pin called “tugbuk” involves inserting pins through the genitals during childhood. The “tugbuk” is used to anchor rings or cogwheels called “sakra.” The purpose was to enhance stimulation during intercourse for their partners.

Pierced Ears

Both men and women wear earrings, with men typically having one or two holes per lobe, and women having three or four. The first few piercings are usually done soon after birth, with additional piercings done before the second year. “Kulot” or “Sipsip” - process for healing torn lobes by trimming the ends and suturing the edges together to heal.

Women typically have more ear piercings per earlobe, allowing for a variety of ornaments. Men's earlobes are stretched larger to accommodate large gold rings that can dangle down to the shoulder. Women find it attractive.

Visayas Tattooing

Visayas tattooing, deeply rooted in the cultural fabric of the Philippine archipelago, showcases a unique blend of symbolism, artistry, and tradition. From the intricate geometric patterns to the symbolic representations of nature and ancestral spirits, each tattoo reflects the rich heritage and identity of the Visayan people.

Kalinga Tattooing

Kalinga tattooing, an ancient art form originating from the Kalinga tribe in the Philippines, holds deep cultural significance within its community. Each symbol and pattern represents a unique narrative, making Kalinga tattooing a revered tradition that continues to thrive amidst modern influences.
